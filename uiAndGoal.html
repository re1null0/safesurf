Design Learnings

To better help understand our users, we created design personas, scenarios and user journey maps. These artifacts condense the raw data we gathered through our research into a tangible summary.

Ideation

"SafeSurf" is an innovative educational platform designed to teach internet safety skills to children aged 9-12. In the digital age, where online interactions are integral to everyday life, educating the younger generation about safe internet practices is crucial. The core objective of "SafeSurf" is to simulate real-world online scenarios, allowing children to experience and navigate potential risks in a controlled, educational environment. This proactive approach in a gamified, interactive setting makes learning both fun and impactful, ensuring that the lessons learned will stick with them as they grow. The ideation process for the "SafeSurf" project was centered around our personal experiences through the lens of our siblings. This process was both iterative and collaborative.
The initial low-fidelity prototype of the SafeSurf app represented an important starting point in our journey to create an engaging and educational platform for children to learn internet safety. At its core, SafeSurf was conceived as a text based simulator, incorporating features like messaging and friend interactions. The prototype featured a straightforward interface with minimal elements. This simplicity was intentional to ensure clarity and ease of use for young users who may not have extensive experience navigating more complex digital interfaces. However, the feedback indicated that some aspects of the interface might be too simplified, which could potentially under-serve the app's educational goals.

Scenario

The persona of Emma Johnson, a typical user of "SafeSurf", provides a deep dive into the needs, behaviors, and goals of children aged 9-12, guiding the development of a platform that is both educational and engaging. Emma represents the curious and enthusiastic segment of middle childhood that is increasingly engaging with digital technology. As a student who particularly enjoys her science and reading classes, Emma's growing interest in using the internet for educational and entertainment purposes is typical of her age. Her activities online include watching videos, playing games, and interacting with peers on social media platforms tailored for children. This early engagement with digital devices underlines the need for robust internet safety education. By age 10, Emma is already comfortable using smartphones and tablets, a testament to the digital nativity of her generation. Supervised usage since she was seven has made her familiar with basic internet navigation. However, her understanding of internet safety nuances remains superficial, highlighting a crucial area for intervention by educational tools like "SafeSurf". Despite her comfort with technology, Emma faces several challenges online. She sometimes encounters situations she's ill-equipped to handle, such as requests for personal information or exposure to inappropriate content. Parental restrictions, while well-intentioned, often cause frustration and may stifle her independent learning. Furthermore, Emma's ability to assess the credibility of online information is still developing, which could lead to misinformation. "SafeSurf" would ideally be integrated into Emma's daily routine, offering her a platform where she can learn to distinguish safe from risky online interactions. The platform aims to simulate real-world internet scenarios in a gamified context, helping Emma and users like her to navigate online spaces safely and confidently. Emma's preferences for interactive, visually appealing content, and hands-on learning align with her developmental stage. She enjoys challenges but often requires encouragement, indicating that "SafeSurf" needs to be supportive and engaging to keep her motivated.

User Needs and User Journey

User journey mapping helped us visualize how a user would interact with a product even before we created a prototype version of it. Moreover it allowed us to see the product from the users perspective to reach the goal. The feedback from evaluators who have experience with younger siblings highlighted several critical needs:
  • Children respond positively to learning when it mimics real-life experiences. Our evaluators noted that the simulation style of the interaction was particularly engaging. This tells us that our design needs to closely mimic real-world social media environments to maintain authenticity and engagement.
  • The addition of progress bars was well-received as it helps users understand how far they have come in their learning journey. This feedback supports the need for visible learning milestones and rewards, which help motivate and guide young users through the educational content.
  • Insights from the evaluations emphasize the importance of effective error handling and clear instructions. Users need to understand what mistakes they've made and how to correct them in a way that is both informative and supportive.
Second user, was a representation of our user group. A 9-year-old girl in 4th grade, provided specific insights that have significantly influenced subsequent iterations of our app. Her feedback also highlighted several areas for improvement.
  • The participant was confused by the simplicity of the home page, questioning why there wasn't more information presented.
  • She expressed curiosity about whether the application offered more functionality beyond the chatbox. This indicates a desire for a more diverse set of features and activities within the app. Her feedback has led us to consider implementing additional interactive elements that could include quizzes, games, and more diverse scenarios, which might help in providing a fuller and more engaging educational experience.
  • The participant noticed that during lulls in the conversation, the chatbot failed to provide engaging and productive prompts. Enhancing the chatbot’s ability to keep the conversation flowing and engaging is essential, especially to maintain the attention of young users and ensure the educational content is delivered effectively.

Finalized User Journey

The finalized user journey for the app starts with Initial Engagement, where users first download and open the application. This stage is critical in forming a good first impression and explaining the app's purpose. In Learning the Basics, users are introduced through a tutorial that outlines key safety lessons, preparing them for more interactive experiences within the app. This is followed by Interactive Simulations, where users apply what they've learned in practical scenarios using a simulator chatbot to teach internet safety actively. The journey culminates in Mastery and Continuous Learning, where users regularly return to engage with new content and refine their settings as they advance, promoting ongoing learning and skill reinforcement. This structured pathway not only educates users about online safety but also encourages continuous engagement, enhancing their ability to safely navigate online environments.

Design Iterations and Evaluation

High Fidelty Prototype 1

    This presents the first iteration of the HiFi Prototype, incorporating advanced features and content learning mechanisms:
  • The user is greeted by a friendly and clean sign-in interface featuring the SafeSurf mascot, enhancing brand recognition and trust. Users can sign in with existing Google or Apple accounts for convenience, reducing barriers to entry.
  • This screen mimics a social media environment where users can interact with friends or simulated accounts. The interface design is intuitive, mirroring popular social media platforms to make the experience familiar for the users.
  • After making decisions, users receive immediate feedback. In the displayed scenario, the user is rewarded for a safe decision with points.

High Fidelity Prototype 2

  • The user is given options to either 'Practice' safe online behaviors or 'Play' interactive scenarios. This choice allows users to engage with the app based on their current comfort and knowledge levels..
  • In the practice module, users are guided on how to respond to common online scenarios correctly. This preparatory step ensures they are equipped before facing actual simulations.
  • The app presents users with real-life situations, like handling friend requests or sharing personal information. Users must choose the safest option, reinforcing learning through practice. users receive immediate feedback. In the displayed scenario, the user is rewarded for a safe decision with points.
  • An interactive chat simulator allows users to apply their knowledge in a controlled environment. Immediate feedback, such as losing a 'heart' for unsafe decisions, helps users learn from their mistakes in real-time.

Moving Forward

Reflecting on the SafeSurf project, several key lessons emerge. First, understanding and addressing user needs through iterative design is fundamental. Second, effective educational tools must go beyond mere content delivery; they must engage, interact, and resonate with their audience. Lastly, the role of clear feedback and visually engaging interfaces cannot be overstated in the context of educational technology. These insights will guide future projects aimed at combining technology with education to create safe, engaging, and effective learning environments for children.
